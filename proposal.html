Problem Statement

Currently, customers have no means of checking allocation on their Metrocards. MTA also fails to provide an equation to the public that allows customers to determine their unique, fair pricing structures for refills.

As a solution to these issues, the app aims to provide MTA NY Subway customers a simple way to calculate prices for convenience and fair, purchasing power. The app also aims to fill the gap of a guiding matrix for customers to make decisions based on value per ride or weekly and monthly passes.

This takes away a lot of the guesswork and alleviates some of the stressors that come with public transportation. Any subway rider can take advantage of the program, including visitors.

Some learning curves of the app will include the customer getting accustomed to checking for their ID. The data will also be hypothetical, since MTA doesn’t provide customers with access to details that the unique ID gives access. Users also have to possess some desire to save on the amount they spend on riding the trains.

User Stories

As a commuter, I want to update my Metrocard with the exact amount necessary because I’m only working three days this week and I don’t want to overspend.

As a tourist, I’m buying swipes for my family of four and we’re all using the same Metrocard. I want to maximize my spending and not have extra money on the card when we leave New York.

As a businessperson, I want to use the Metrocard I have from my last business trip, but don’t know how much is on it, or how much I should put on it for my two-day conference. I’m only going to meetings, and back to my hotel. How much should I put on?

User Testing

Design

In order to create Metrocalc, our team analyzed our user persona information and kept them in mind throughout the design process. We designed mobile-first in order to maintain cross-device functionality, and embrace a minimal, flexible layout. At it’s core, the interface is a calculator which takes the information from your Metrocard account and determines the most efficient spending with regard to adding rides. When a user enters the site, they’ll only be introduced with the site’s mission and a place to enter their Metrocard ID#. Once the user submits their number, their balance, card expiration, and ride add features will be visible.

By segmenting the output of information, we’re reducing the user’s cognitive load and only giving them the information that they need. Through our research we found that users needed fast, reliable, and easy to use tools for adding value to their Metrocards, so we chose to use a ride counter that would add rides and formulate the new price simultaneously. Once the user entered their ID#, they would only have to choose how many rides they wanted, and press buy – a minimum of two clicks before reaching checkout.

Based on our research, we realized our users might want to refill their card with time-based amounts as well. Our team integrated a “Buy 7-day unlimited pass” and “Buy 30-day unlimited pass” options underneath our per-trip calculator so users could easily make a single click before reaching the checkout.

As an added bonus to our site, the team included an MTA Twitter feed to give users a sense of community and update them to any major transit news.

Implementation

To program our digital prototypes and visual designs, our team relied on a combination of HTML, CSS, JavaScript and JQuery. The majority of our interface is completely functional, with only the cards themselves being hardcoded into the JavaScript. The calculator aspect of the interface itself was written in JavaScript, and the Twitter feed was implemented using JQuery. There were a few implementation issues that were out of scope, including linking the calculator output value to the subsequent cart page. Another option that we could have done regarding the plus and minus input was to create a matrix of clickable options, each communicating a different price value for any combination of rides you wanted to take. For our MVP we decided that the plus and minus counter would be more than sufficient and allow our users sufficient freedom in customizing their journey. One user experience aspect that would be implemented in a future version is to allow users to press enter after they input their Metrocard ID# instead of clicking the submit button.

Evaluation

MetroCalc is a tool to check the status of one’s NYC metrocard. In order to test it, our team of 3 usability professionals reached out to residents of NYC that utilizes the subway frequently. The users ranged in age and profession. One common factor was that the tester was a customer to the NYC subways. The users asked to walk through the following 5 tasks:

  1. Check how much value is left on your Metrocard
    • User must input the Metrocard’s ID number (123)
    • User must submit the info
  2. Check how much time is left on your Metrocard
    • User must input the Metrocard’s ID number (000)
    • User must submit the info
  3. Calculate how much value to add for a specific amount of rides on your Metrocard
    • User must input the Metrocard’s ID number (123)
    • User must submit the info
    • User should scroll to see their pricing matrix
  4. Calculate how much value to add for a specific amount of rides on your Metrocard
    • User must input the Metrocard’s ID number (000)
    • User must submit the info
    • User should scroll to see their pricing matrix
    • User can choose the value
    • User adds value to cart
    • User pays and checks out; the time/value is added to their card
  5. Check MTA updates on Twitter
    • User must agree to terms and conditions
    • User opens app to see Twitter feed

With the 5 tasks, the users were asked to go through the steps by thinking out loud to reflect the user's’ thinking process as a NYC subway user. The users were given two pre-determined metrocard IDs to mimic the experience of entering the metrocard number in the application.

Reflection

Problem 1: User kept trying to click enter when submitting card numbers. ​

Solution: Implement javascript event listener for enter button when user submits card number.

Problem 2: User ended up clicking a dozen times to change the amount of rides they needed. User complained that it was inefficient to keep tapping to get to the number you need.

Solution: Add a visual price matrix that shows users side-by-side ride/cost information instead of having them click a bunch of times. Then users would be able to see the larger picture instead of small increments.

Problem 3: User found the wording of “How many rides will you take?” confusing. She didn’t realize it she is able to add and pay for that amount of rides until she saw the “Add $x.xx” button. ​

Solution: Reword “how many rides will you take?” to something like “How many rides do you want to add to your card”.

Problem 4: User was confused moving from “Enter your MetroCard ID number to determine your balance.” to see the amount and time on card and “How many rides will you take?”

Solution: Clearly mark that “Enter your MetroCard ID” section and “how many rides will you take” section to be different sections through color or thicker breaker line.

Problem 5: ​U​ser mentioned that what is missing is a hint on how and where to find the specific ID on a metrocard. This is similar to how payment forms guide you to find credit card numbers on cards. For example, credit card payment forms offer you a hint to find the CCV number on the back. ​

Solution: They offered a suggestion to employ a similar tactic to help new customers find the ID number.

Problem 6: User also mentioned that some riders/customers don’t understand the full restrictions of Unlimited passes. They noted that pay-per-rides offer more flexibility. For example, you can pay for multiple rides, one after the other, versus having to wait 15 minutes between swipes on an Unlimited card.

Solution: FAQ page or an FYI near the ‘Buy’ buttons.
